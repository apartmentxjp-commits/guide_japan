How to Buy an Akiya (Vacant House) in Japan: Complete Guide for Foreigners

January 15, 2026 · 4 min read ·
akiya vacant house japan real estate buy property japan rural japan

Japan has an extraordinary opportunity hiding in plain sight: over 9 million vacant homes (akiya) scattered across the country, many available for as little as ¥1–5 million (roughly $7,000–$35,000 USD). Here’s how foreigners can buy one.

What Is an Akiya?

Akiya (空き家) literally means “empty house” in Japanese. These are properties left vacant due to Japan’s aging population, rural depopulation, and inheritance complications. Many are structurally sound homes in scenic rural areas — abandoned simply because owners moved to cities or passed away without heirs willing to maintain them.

Key facts about Japan’s akiya situation:

  • Over 9 million vacant homes as of 2023 (government data)
  • Number growing by approximately 350,000 per year
  • Many municipalities are practically giving them away to attract new residents
  • Some prefectures offer subsidies of ¥500,000–¥2,000,000 for renovation

Can Foreigners Buy Property in Japan?

Yes. Japan has no restrictions on foreign ownership of real estate. Whether you’re a permanent resident, visa holder, or even a non-resident, you can purchase property. You don’t need a visa to buy — though you’ll need one to live there.

This makes Japan one of the most foreigner-friendly real estate markets in Asia.

How to Buy an Akiya: Step-by-Step

Step 1: Find an Akiya

The main search methods:

  1. Akiya Banks — municipal websites listing vacant properties (often free or very cheap)
  2. Japan Akiya Portal — English-language listing aggregator
  3. Real estate agents (fudosan) — local agents who specialize in rural properties
  4. Word of mouth — ask locals in the area you’re interested in

Step 2: Visit and Inspect the Property

Don’t buy without visiting. Old Japanese houses can have:

  • Termite damage (especially wooden structures)
  • Foundation issues in earthquake-prone areas
  • Asbestos (common in pre-1989 construction)
  • Outdated electrical systems (100V, older wiring)

Hire a qualified building inspector (建物調査士) — costs approximately ¥50,000–¥100,000.

Step 3: Make an Offer Through an Agent

In Japan, all real estate transactions must go through a licensed real estate agent (宅地建物取引士). The agent:

  • Handles the purchase agreement (売買契約書)
  • Conducts the “Important Matters Explanation” (重要事項説明)
  • Manages the title transfer (所有権移転登記)

Agent fees: Typically 3% of purchase price + ¥60,000 + consumption tax.

Step 4: Sign the Contract and Pay

Japan typically uses a two-stage contract process:

  1. Sales agreement (売買契約) — paid deposit (usually 10% of price)
  2. Final closing (残代金決済) — pay remaining balance, receive keys

Step 5: Register the Property

After closing, your agent or a judicial scrivener (司法書士) will register the property in your name at the local Legal Affairs Bureau. Cost: approximately ¥100,000–¥200,000.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a Japanese bank account to buy property? Yes — for domestic transactions, you’ll need a Japanese bank account to transfer funds. Opening one requires a residence card (在留カード) or specific bank types that accept non-residents.

Can I rent out my akiya? Yes. Short-term rentals (minpaku) require a license under Japan’s Minpaku Law. Long-term rentals are generally simpler to arrange.

Are there any akiya with subsidies? Yes — many municipalities offer cash subsidies of ¥500,000–¥2,000,000 for renovation, plus some offer free land transfer if you agree to renovate and live there for a set period.
